    Calcium acetate & phosphorus
    
        
    Educational reference — not medical advice
    
      
    
      
    
        
    
          Calcium acetate is a mealtime phosphate binder: it pairs with phosphorus in the gut so less is absorbed. It is commonly used when blood phosphate runs high (often with kidney disease). Most over-the-counter calcium acetate is synthesized because food sources never reach binder-strength concentrations.
        
    
        
    
          
    Dosing expectancies
    
          
      
            
    • About 45 mg of phosphorus bound per 1 g of calcium acetate.
      • 
            
    • A typical mealtime dose of 2 tablets (1,334 mg total calcium acetate) binds roughly 60–70 mg of phosphorus from that meal.
      • 
            
    • Binders work on phosphate in the meal you take them with—timing and tablet count are usually set by a clinician for your labs, not guessed from diet tracking alone.
      • 
          
    
        
    
        
    
          What phosphorus does in the body
          
    
            
      
              
    • Energy (ATP): every cell uses phosphate to store and release energy.
      • 
              
    • Genes & repair: DNA and RNA backbones are phosphate-based; you need phosphorus to build and copy cells.
      • 
              
    • Bone: hydroxyapatite crystals in bone are a calcium–phosphate matrix—about 85% of body phosphorus sits in skeleton.
      • 
              
    • Membranes & signaling: phospholipids form cell membranes; phosphate groups switch proteins on and off.
      • 
              
    • Acid–base balance: phosphate buffers help keep blood pH in range.
      • 
            
    
            
    Whole-food phosphorus from meat, dairy, beans, and nuts supports these roles; the calcification concern is usually excess absorbable phosphate from additives, not meeting basic needs.
    
          
    
        
    
        
    
          If phosphorus drops too low
          
    
            
      
              
    • Early signs can include weakness, loss of appetite, and bone or joint discomfort.
      • 
              
    • More severe deficiency can affect breathing muscles, cause confusion, and disrupt heart rhythm—this is a medical emergency territory.
      • 
              
    • Aggressive binder use without monitoring is the usual context for low phosphate, not simply eating less processed food.
      • 
              
    • Do not use phosphate binders to “balance” a normal diet; if you have kidney disease, elevated phosphate, or take binders already, dose changes belong with your care team and lab work.

    Caffeine gum, patches & mineral absorption
    
        
    Why delivery method can matter for micronutrients
    
      
    
      
    
        
    Caffeine gum or patches can be a cleaner way to get caffeine than coffee or tea. You still get the stimulant, but you skip the plant compounds that can get in the way of absorbing minerals from food.
    
        
    Tannins and polyphenols in coffee and tea can bind iron, zinc, calcium, and magnesium in your gut before they are absorbed—iron is hit hardest. Coffee with a meal has been shown to reduce iron uptake by about 39%; tea, about 64%. Tea also adds oxalate, which is worth noting if you are prone to kidney stones.
    
        
    Gum delivers some caffeine through the mouth lining and some through digestion; patches bypass most of the gut. Both avoid the main coffee-and-tea problem. Caffeine itself can still slightly increase how much calcium and magnesium you lose in urine, so it is not a free pass—just a different tradeoff.
    
      
    
      
    
        
      
    
    
    
  
    

  
    
    
    
    
    
      
    
        
    Eggs, cholesterol & heart health
    
        
    Why saturated fat matters more than dietary cholesterol
    
      
    
      
    
        
    For most healthy adults, eating eggs in moderation is not considered bad for your heart. Egg yolks are high in cholesterol, but modern research shows dietary cholesterol has much less impact on blood cholesterol levels than saturated fats.
    
        
    Eggs are nutritional powerhouses with several cardiovascular benefits. They are an excellent source of high-quality protein, vitamins D and B12, choline, and lutein. The unsaturated fats in eggs can actually raise HDL (good cholesterol), which helps protect your blood vessels.
    
        
    Despite being a high-cholesterol food, eggs are low in saturated fat—about 1.5 grams per large egg. Saturated fats, not dietary cholesterol alone, are the primary driver of high blood cholesterol for most people.
    
      
    
      
    
        
      
    
    
    
  
    

  
    
    
    
    
    
      
    
        
    TMAO protectors
    
        
    How diet lowers trimethylamine N-oxide beyond cutting precursors
    
      
    
      
    
        
    TMAO is not in food. Gut microbes first make TMA (trimethylamine) from choline, carnitine, and betaine; your liver then converts TMA into TMAO, which promotes atherosclerosis. Protectors work in three ways; none replace eating less carnitine-rich red meat, but together they explain why plant-rich and Mediterranean-style eaters often run lower TMAO.
    
        
    1. Block the bacterial TMA-lyase enzyme
    
        
    These act on the bug that converts precursors to TMA—before the liver can turn TMA into TMAO—not just on how much meat you eat.
    
        
      
          
    • Extra-virgin olive oil — contains DMB (3,3-dimethyl-1-butanol), which in animal studies inhibits gut bacteria from turning choline and carnitine into TMAO; a core reason Mediterranean diets correlate with lower TMAO.
      • 
          
    • Monounsaturated fat (olive & avocado oil) — tracked above; olive oil is the main food source of DMB.
      • 
          
    • Balsamic & red-wine vinegar — DMB and polyphenols from wine-derived vinegars.
      • 
          
    • Grapeseed oil — another DMB-containing oil in research.
      • 
          
    • Raw garlic — allicin inhibits TMA-lyase; cooking reduces allicin—crush or chop and let sit before heat, or use raw in dressings.
      • 
        
    
        
    2. Reshape the microbiome away from TMA producers
    
        
    Shifts gut populations so less TMA forms from precursors—and less TMAO follows once the liver processes it. Changes take weeks, not overnight.
    
        
      
          
    • Fiber & prebiotics — oats, beans, lentils, vegetables, whole grains; soluble fiber may also lower LDL. Vegetarians often produce far less TMAO even when choline intake is adequate.
      • 
          
    • Polyphenols — feed beneficial gut microbes and curb bacteria that convert choline and carnitine into TMA; the liver then oxidizes TMA into TMAO, the compound tied to atherosclerosis. Berries, tea, cocoa, coffee, onions, olive oil, dark chocolate.
      • 
          
    • Flavonoids — a polyphenol subclass with the same microbiome-shifting role; berries, tea, citrus, onions, kale, apples, parsley.
      • 
          
    • Resveratrol — grapes, blueberries, cranberries, peanuts, pistachios; in animal studies lowers TMAO by remodeling gut bacteria (raising Akkermansia and bile-acid signaling)—food doses are modest, so treat as one of several plant compounds.
      • 
        
    
        
    3. Help the body process and clear TMAO
    
        
      
          
    • Vitamin D — some human trials and animal studies show supplementation lowers TMAO, possibly by shifting gut microbes, especially when you start deficient.
      • 
          
    • Folate (B9), vitamin B12, and vitamin B6 — in a one-year trial, vitamin D plus these B vitamins lowered fasting TMAO more than vitamin D alone—likely through homocysteine and choline metabolism cross-talk; correcting deficiency matters more than megadoses.
      • 
          
    • EPA & DHA (fatty fish / fish oil) — supports vascular health on the downstream side of TMAO risk; tracked as EPA + DHA above.
      • 
        
    
        
    Dietary patterns & fermented foods
    
        
    There is no single “TMAO reversal pill.” Mediterranean-style eating—low red meat, olive oil, vinegar, legumes, fish, lots of plants—reliably lowers TMAO in trials. Probiotics and fermented foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut) may help but are strain-specific and hard to count in this dashboard—eat them regularly rather than chasing numbers.
    
      
    
      
    
        
      
    
    
    
  
    

  
    
    
    
    
    
      
    
        
    Fiber & colorectal cancer prevention
    
        
    Why % DV matters for colon health
    
      
    
      
    
        
    Dietary fiber is one of the most consistent dietary factors linked to lower colorectal cancer risk in large population studies—not a guarantee, but a practical prevention lever you can track every week.
    
        
    What the research suggests
    
        
      
          
    • ~10% lower risk per 10 g/day — meta-analyses estimate each additional 10 g of total fiber per day is associated with roughly 10% lower colorectal cancer risk (WCRF/AICR evidence).
      • 
          
    • High vs. low fiber diets — people eating the most fiber from whole grains, legumes, vegetables, and fruit often show about 15–20% lower risk than those eating the least, after adjusting for other lifestyle factors.
      • 
          
    • Whole grains & legumes — insoluble fiber adds bulk and shortens transit time through the colon; soluble fiber feeds beneficial gut bacteria that produce protective short-chain fatty acids like butyrate.
      • 
        
    
        
    How to use this dashboard
    
        
    Track fiber on your micro entries. The longevity panel shows your weekly average as % DV (about 38 g/day for men, 25 g/day for women—see Settings). Aim for 100%+ DV from food: beans, lentils, oats, barley, berries, broccoli, whole-grain bread, chia, and flax are reliable sources. Raise intake gradually and drink enough water to avoid bloating.
    
        
    This tool is educational—not a substitute for colonoscopy screening or medical advice. If you have a family history of colorectal cancer or digestive symptoms, follow your clinician’s screening schedule.

    Nutrition labels often show one genderless % DV, but needs differ in practice:
    
            
      
              
    • Iron: Women typically need more iron (about 18 mg/day) than men (about 8 mg/day), largely due to menstruation.
      • 
              
    • Vitamins & fiber: Men generally need higher daily intakes of some vitamins (e.g. vitamin C, zinc) and fiber because of higher average body mass and calorie needs.
      • 
              
    • Calories: A common maintenance baseline is roughly 2,400–2,600 cal/day for men and 1,800–2,000 cal/day for women (this app’s week calories still come from your settings, not these baselines).

    TDEE comparison
    
        
    Using intake vs maintenance for body-composition goals
    
      
    
      
    
        
    Compare your logged week total intake to your weekly maintenance (TDEE × 7) to lean down (calorie deficit) or gain mass (calorie surplus). Pair the direction with a macro split that fits your goal—higher protein often helps whether you are cutting or bulking.
    
        
    Rule of thumb: about 3,500 kcal of cumulative surplus or deficit over time corresponds to roughly 1 lb of body-weight change on average. That energy-balance relationship holds whether the tissue gained or lost is mostly fat or lean mass—macros influence body composition, not the calorie arithmetic itself.
    
        
    Individual results vary with metabolism, activity, sleep, stress, and consistency. This is a planning aid, not medical advice.
